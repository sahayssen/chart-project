Anti-Mamdani Groups Dominated Independent Spending in 2025

These are the groups that tried to shape this year's mayoral race

By Sophie Hayssen | November 30, 2025

Over the course of former New York Governor Andrew Cuomo’s failed mayoral campaign in New York City, he received a lot of press for being the preferred candidate for billionaires, like Bill Ackman and James Dolan. Analysis of independent expenditures supports these claims. However, rather than just supporting Cuomo, independent spenders were especially focused on opposing Cuomo’s opponent Zohran Mamdani and, to a lesser extent, the longshot Republican candidate Curtis Sliwa.

Groups, including the race’s top spenders, contributed around $12 million to anti-Mamdani efforts and around $8 million to support Cuomo. Pro-Mamdani spending totaled less than $4 million dollars with a negligible amount of anti-Cuomo spending at only $532.

The top three spenders included Stop the Socialists, Fix the City, and Defend NYC. Stop the Socialists donated significant amounts to support Cuomo and oppose Mamdani and Sliwa; Fix the City was the only group among the top three who donated more to support Cuomo than oppose Mamdani; DefendNYC, like the next highest spenders, contributed significantly more to anti-Mamdani efforts than to support Cuomo. The trend of anti-Mamdani groups spending larger amounts isn’t just true in general. Granular data, looking at day today spending showed anti-Mamdani spenders often gave larger amounts, which increased significantly in the last few weeks before the general election.

While all spending increased in late October, pro-Mamdani spending, which tended to be in smaller amounts, spread out across the race. Both pro and anti-Mamdani spending focused on TV and Cable, although Mamdani opponents spent nearly a million more than his supporters. There was no top media category where Mamdani supporters outspent his opponents, especially in categories like text messaging and leaflets and flyers. Pro-Cuomo and anti-Mamdani camps spent over a million dollars on text messages and over two million on flyers, while Mamdani spenders paid less that $100,000 for those media.

Chart showing how spending varies across position and media. Anti-Mamdani supporters particularly favored TV/Cable ads.

Mamdani ultimately won the general election with just over 50% of the vote . Potential takeaways from his campaign is that grassroots power, echoed in the pro-Mamdani camp’s smaller but more numerous spending, can counter steeply priced, concentrated campaigns like the one to oppose Mamdani. The plethora of anti-Mamdani ads and campaigns compared to the nearly nonexistent anti-Cuomo spending also signals that negative campaigns don’t always work. The pro-Mamdani camp only needed to harness their relatively few resources to make positive content about their candidate to achieve victory. Experts at the New York Campaign Finance Board did not respond to my request for comment.
